2 - Where are the busiest areas in Engagement? Are there traffic bottlenecks that should be addressed? Explain your rationale. Limit your response to 10 images and 500 words.

Overview

Analyzing movement patterns reveals concentrated activity along specific corridors connecting residential and commercial zones. Temporal analysis shows distinct peak periods when roads experience maximum usage, creating potential bottlenecks that could benefit from infrastructure improvements.

Overall Activity Density

The heatmap of all participant locations throughout the study period reveals clear high-density zones. The busiest areas concentrate in downtown, with secondary activity centers on the northwest mixed zone and eastern residential-commercial zone. Major connecting corridors between these zones show sustained high usage throughout the day.

Figure 2.1. Business hours activity heatmap showing busiest areas during typical workday hours.

Rush Hour Movement Patterns

Morning rush hours (7-9 AM) exhibit concentrated movement from residential zones in the peripheral zones toward employment centers in downtown. The roads connecting these areas become primary bottlenecks, particularly the single connecting route to the northwest zone.

Figure 2.2. Morning rush hour (7-9 AM) activity showing concentrated movement toward employment centers.

Evening rush hours (5-7 PM) reverse this pattern, with heavy traffic from commercial zones back to residential areas. The same corridors experience bidirectional bottlenecks, compounded by social activity beginning simultaneously as work ends.

Figure 2.3. Evening rush hour (5-7 PM) showing return traffic to residential zones plus social activity.

Activity Type Analysis

The streamgraph visualization reveals temporal patterns across a general day explaining the bottleneck. Work-related activity dominates weekday daytime hours (8 AM-6 PM), while social/recreation activities peak during evenings and weekends. Eating-related movement shows trimodal patterns around meal times, creating predictable congestion at 8 AM, 12 PM, and 6-7 PM.

Figure 2.4. Temporal distribution of activity modes showing work, social, and eating patterns throughout a day.

Critical Bottleneck Routes

Three critical bottlenecks emerge from the analysis:

Route 1 (Northwest Connector): The single major road connecting the northwest zone to the central city creates severe congestion during peak hours. This route serves a substantial residential and commercial population with no alternative paths, making it the most critical bottleneck in the city.

Figure 2.5. Northwest connector route showing heavy single-path dependency during morning commute.

Route 2 (Downtown Connector): In downtown we can also identify some smaller bottleneck routes, as most people tend to pass through these roads to reach their destination, leading to persistent congestion.

Figure 2.6. Central connector showing persistent congestion during peak hours.

Route 3 (Southern Connector): Roads connecting the southeastern zones to the central downtown create bottlenecks during both morning and evening rush hours, particularly where multiple residential areas converge.

Figure 2.7. Southern connector route showing bottlenecks during peak hours.

Temporal Congestion Patterns

Observing the heatmap on the weekend, the heatmap is much more uniform, not showing big bottlenecks. In general you can see bigger clusters near pubs and restaurants, reflecting social activity patterns.

Figure 2.8. Weekend activity patterns showing more uniform distribution and social clusters.

Summary

The busiest areas concentrate in downtown with critical bottlenecks on routes connecting peripheral residential areas to this downtown core. The northwest connector represents the most severe single-point failure, while the downtown corridor experiences sustained congestion. Infrastructure improvements should prioritize creating alternative routes and distributing commercial activity to reduce pressure on these critical bottlenecks.

Participant Selection

Two participants were selected displaying contrasting patterns: Participant 266 representing a socially active individual with short commutes and high joviality, and Participant 845 representing a long-distance commuter with limited social activity and much lower joviality.

Their home and workplace are located in close proximity in the city center, enabling easy walking-distance commutes. They have one of the highest joviality scores (97.3%) and maintain robust social activity (18.3% of time).

Figure 3.2. Summary statistics for Participant 266 highlighting travel distance, joviality, and social activity.

The activity calendar visualization provides detailed temporal insights across the month of March 2022. Participant 266 displays highly regular weekday work patterns with consistent start and end times. Their work schedule shows minimal variation, appearing as solid blocks in the calendar view. Right after work the participant would get straight into socializing near the neighborhood, occasionally needing to take transportation for not more than 1 hour to do so. Participant 845: The Long-Distance Commuter

Participant 845 is a 40 year old person who presents a contrasting profile, spending 13.6% of his time traveling on average, substantially above the sample mean. Their home in the south of Engagement while his workplace is on the east side, necessitating long commutes traversing multiple bottleneck routes for 32,2 units per day (6 times more than Participant 266). They have below-average joviality and maintain limited social engagement, averaging only 5.2% of social activity on March 2022.

Figure 3.4. Summary statistics for Participant 845 highlighting travel distance, joviality, and social activity.

The map reveals the geographic challenges they face. The substantial distance between home (south) and work (east) requires traversing congested corridors identified as major bottlenecks. Social activities, when they occur, involve even longer journeys.

The activity calendar reveals their demanding daily routine. Weekday mornings begin with departure from home at 7:00 AM, followed by an 1 hour long commute, arriving at work around 8:00 AM. They finish work around 16:00 PM, facing another extended commute that brings them home just after 17:00 PM. Most evenings are spent at home, with one/two social evenings per week.

Figure 3.5. Activity calendar for Participant 845 showing consistent long commutes (red), regular work blocks (orange), and limited social activity (green).

Comparative Analysis

The contrast between these participants highlights how geographic location relative to workplace and amenities profoundly impacts daily experience. Participant 266's localized activity pattern enables high social engagement and likely contributes to sustained well-being. Participant 845's long-distance commute consumes substantial daily time, constraining opportunities for social connection and potentially contributing to lower initial joviality.

Summary

These two participants exemplify the spectrum of daily experiences in Engagement. Participant 266 benefits from proximity between home, work, and social venues, enabling an active social life with minimal travel burden. Participant 845 faces geographic constraints requiring extensive daily commuting through congested routes, limiting social opportunities and consuming significant time. These patterns underscore the importance of urban planning that considers residential-workplace proximity and transportation infrastructure in supporting quality of life.

 

4 - Over the span of the dataset, how do patterns change in the city change? Describe up to 10 significant changes, with supporting evidence. Limit your response to 10 images and 500 words.

Overview

Change 1: Initial Adjustment Period and Activity Normalization

The study's opening 60 days reveal the most substantial behavioral shift. Traffic volumes begin notably elevated during the first week, then progressively diminish over two months before reaching stable equilibrium levels that persist throughout the remaining observation period. This trajectory indicates an initial novelty effect or heightened participant engagement that gradually normalizes into representative behavioral patterns.

Change 2: Early Social Activity Surge

Individual activity calendars expose concentrated social engagement during the opening 3-4 weeks across numerous participants. Participants such as 266 and 797 demonstrate markedly elevated social event frequency before transitioning to more sustainable routine patterns. This behavioral shift substantially contributes to the elevated traffic observed in Change 1, since social activities typically necessitate greater travel distances compared to habitual work-home movements.

Figure 4.2. Activity calendar for Participant 797 showing intense social activity in opening weeks before stabilizing to regular patterns.

Change 3: Cyclical Behavioral Variations

Following the initial stabilization phase, participants exhibit recurring fluctuations in social and dining behaviors across the 15-month span. These manifest as episodic activity bursts, encompassing weekend routine alterations and evening behavior modifications. Participant 744 exemplifies this with multiple discrete periods of behavioral adjustment, suggesting lifestyle adaptations, seasonal influences, or circumstantial changes affecting cohorts simultaneously.

Figure 4.3. Activity calendar for Participant 744 showing periodic bursts of behavioral change in social and eating activities.

Change 4: Interest Group Homogeneity in Urban Behavior

Analysis of the 10 distinct interest groups (A-J) reveals minimal behavioral divergence in temporal and spatial patterns. The heatmap with interest group filtering demonstrates that all groups exhibit nearly identical spatial distributions at corresponding times, suggesting shared infrastructure usage regardless of demographic affiliation. Activity calendars across different group members similarly show comparable daily rhythms and weekly patterns. This homogeneity indicates that urban spatial constraints and social norms produce convergent behaviors that override demographic differences. The lack of observable interest group-specific patterns suggests either that the grouping criteria doesn't meaningfully segment behavioral types, or that the city's functional geography enforces universal patterns across all demographic segments.

Figure 4.4. Spatial distribution comparison across different interest groups revealing similar patterns rather than distinctive behaviors.

Change 5: Activity Type Composition Uniform Over Study Duration

The streamgraph doesn't reveal that activity type proportions evolve particularly throughout the study period. It shows balanced activity distribution, with increasing work activity proportion relative to social engagement during the weekdays. It does show tho that the number of participants decreses over time, altough the percentage distribution of activities remains roughly the same.

Figure 4.5. Streamgraph comparison across three study periods showing evolution in activity type proportions over 15 months.

Change 6: Functional Weekend Transformation

Weekend activity composition differs fundamentally from weekday patterns. Morning movement at 08:00 on weekdays consists predominantly of employment-related travel, while equivalent Saturday morning traffic comprises social and dining-oriented journeys. This functional transformation redistributes traffic spatially, with distinct routes experiencing demand peaks contingent on temporal cycles.

Change 7: Individual Behavioral Signature Persistence

Despite population-level pattern changes, individual activity calendars demonstrate remarkable personal routine consistency. Participants maintain distinctive "behavioral fingerprints" - characteristic weekly schedules that persist across the study duration. Participant 266 consistently shows Wednesday evening social peaks, while participant 744 exhibits Friday-Saturday late-night patterns throughout all 15 months. This individual-level stability contrasts with aggregate volatility, suggesting observed population changes reflect participant dropout, cohort turnover, or shifting composition rather than individual behavioral modification. Previous streamgraph pictures highlight the composition of activities on the global level, while activity calendars reveal individual-level persistence.

Change 8: Spatial Clustering Evolution by Activity Type

The heatmap's building overlay reveals evolving relationships between activity types and urban geography. Early study weeks show eating and social activities dispersed across numerous venues, but concentration increases progressively as participants discover preferred locations. By month 6, social activity concentrates in 3-4 distinct hotspots, while eating activity consolidates around 8-10 popular venues. Work locations remain stable throughout, but recreational activities show seasonal geographic shifts, with southern zones gaining prominence in warmer months (June-September) before reverting to indoor northern venues.

Figure 4.8. Spatial distribution comparison showing progressive concentration of social activities from dispersed (early) to clustered (mature) patterns.

Change 9: Multi-Activity Simultaneity in Dense Zones

The streamgraph's stacked representation reveals that certain time periods exhibit remarkable activity diversity, with 5-6 different activity types occurring simultaneously at comparable intensities. This multi-activity pattern peaks during evening hours (18:00-21:00) and weekend afternoons, when work activities diminish but no single leisure activity dominates. The spatial heatmap confirms this corresponds to central mixed-use zones where diverse venue types cluster. In contrast, morning hours show activity homogeneity with work-related movement overwhelming all other activities, concentrating spatially in dedicated employment districts.

Change 10: Emergence and Decay of Venue Popularity

The heatmap's temporal progression reveals dynamic venue popularity cycles that traditional static analysis would miss. Certain locations exhibit sudden activity surges lasting 2-4 weeks before declining to baseline levels, suggesting temporary popularity driven by word-of-mouth or special events. Multiple venues show this "flash popularity" pattern sequentially, indicating participants progressively explore different social destinations. Conversely, some initially popular venues experience permanent decline after month 3, while others maintain steady traffic throughout the study. Activity calendars corroborate this at the individual level, showing participants transitioning between favorite venues over time rather than maintaining fixed location preferences.

Figure 4.10. Heatmap time series showing temporal evolution of activity concentration at specific venues, revealing popularity emergence and decay patterns.

Summary

The 15-month investigation reveals multi-scale behavioral dynamics operating simultaneously at individual, group, and population levels. Individual activity calendars demonstrate persistent personal routines despite population volatility, while interest group analysis reveals behavioral homogeneity across demographic segments—suggesting urban spatial constraints override demographic differences. Activity type composition evolves substantially across the study duration, with streamgraph analysis revealing progressive work-life balance shifts and spatial clustering patterns that mature over time. Dynamic venue popularity cycles emerge from temporal heatmap analysis, showing progressive exploration and shifting preferences. The combination of temporal (streamgraph), spatial (heatmap), and individual (activity calendar) visualizations enables detection of changes invisible to single-perspective analysis, demonstrating that urban behavioral patterns exhibit both remarkable stability at micro-scales and substantial evolution at macro-scales.
